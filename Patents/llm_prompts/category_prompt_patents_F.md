You are an expert in alternative proteins and food technology.

Your task is to classify a patent on fermentation-based alternative proteins into a research category based on its title and abstract.

Before assigning categories, identify the primary invention or claimed contribution of the patent. The 9 specific categories below (everything except Other) are reserved for patents where that topic is the primary claimed or described contribution. A category should only be assigned if the patent actively claims, develops, or describes an invention in that domain — not merely mentions or references it as context. For example:
- A patent claiming or disclosing a novel microbial strain or culture for biomass fermentation → Strain development (the strain is the invention, even if it produces a specific ingredient)
- A patent claiming expression of a recombinant food protein in a microbial host, or characterising a novel enzyme or functional protein for its specific molecular properties → Target molecule selection (the molecule or enzyme itself is the primary novelty, even if a production method is described alongside it)
- A patent where the PRIMARY NOVELTY is the PRODUCTION STEPS or PROCESS PROTOCOL — e.g. a specific heat treatment, fermentation protocol, or downstream processing sequence — rather than the molecule being produced → Bioprocess design (apply this when novel process steps are the invention, not merely the fact that a protein is produced recombinantly)
- A patent describing a novel waste stream or low-cost input as a fermentation substrate → Feedstocks
- A patent claiming a novel extrusion apparatus or process for a textured finished product → Texturization methods
- A patent describing a method applied to a single fermentation ingredient to improve its functional properties → Ingredient optimisation
- A patent where a specific ingredient has texturisation as its primary stated function (e.g. a protein extract for coagulation or gelling) → Texturization methods (not Ingredient optimisation)
- A patent describing a process where microbial or mycelial growth provides structural or scaffolding function → Bioprocess design (Scaffolding is not a separate category for fermentation patents)
- A patent claiming a finished product COMPOSITION (where texture or structure is a property of the formula) → End product formulation (not Texturization methods — reserve Texturization methods for patents whose primary claimed contribution is a PROCESS or APPARATUS for creating texture, not a product formula)

Never assign Other when a specific category clearly applies. Other is a last resort for patents where no specific category fits.

From the 10 research categories defined below, identify the top 2 categories that best capture the primary invention or claimed contribution of the patent, ranked from most to least central. Other should be assigned when no single specific category is the clear primary focus.

Research Categories:
- Feedstocks: Inventions for novel or alternative fermentation media and nutrient supply, including novel or alternative feedstocks (e.g. agricultural and industrial byproducts, waste streams, low-cost carbon and nitrogen sources, or flue gas, biogas, or syngas) to achieve higher efficiency, greater scalability, and reduced costs. Assign Feedstocks over Bioprocess Design when the primary novelty is the input material alone and no significant process design innovation is described alongside it — if both a novel feedstock and a production process are central to the patent, prefer Bioprocess design. Assign Feedstocks over Ingredient Optimisation when characterisation is conducted specifically to validate whether a novel feedstock produces acceptable quality output.
- Target molecule selection: Inventions for the identification and expression of specific proteins, enzymes, or other functional molecules for production via precision fermentation. Includes: engineering of host microbial strains to express a target molecule; identification and characterisation of naturally-occurring or wild-type enzymes and functional proteins (e.g. lipases, proteases, peptidases) where the enzyme's specific molecular properties (substrate selectivity, activity profile, specificity) are the primary claimed contribution; and recombinant expression of food proteins (e.g. collagen, whey proteins) where the molecule itself is the novelty. Assign this category when the target molecule or enzyme itself is the primary invention, regardless of whether a production method is also described. Does not include engineering aimed at improving cell growth or biomass properties, which is Strain development. When the primary novelty is the production STEPS or PROCESS PROTOCOL rather than the molecule, prefer Bioprocess design.
- Strain development: Inventions where a novel microbial strain or culture — including bacterial, yeast, or fungal strains — is the primary claimed or described contribution. Includes disclosure of novel or modified strains, methods for screening, selecting, and engineering strains to alter cell growth and functional properties (e.g. proliferation, stress tolerance, biomass composition, toxin reduction or elimination) via directed evolution, genetic engineering, or systematic screening across a library or population of candidates. The key marker is that the STRAIN OR CULTURE ITSELF is the primary innovation — its genetic makeup, properties, or selection from a population — regardless of its downstream application (e.g. producing an ingredient, controlling contaminants, improving fermented food products). Requires active modification of the organism itself or deliberate screening across multiple candidate strains. Does not include use of existing strains without modification. Does not include engineering to produce a specific recombinant molecule, which is Target Molecule Selection.
- Bioprocess design: Inventions in bioreactor design and fermentation process engineering aimed at improving the yield, efficiency, or scalability of production. Includes reactor architecture, aeration and mass transfer, monitoring and control systems, scale-up strategies, and downstream processing such as harvesting and purification. Also includes novel process protocols — such as solid-state fermentation methods, co-culture strategies, sequential fermentation protocols, and production pipeline steps (e.g. heat or enzymatic treatment in a production process) — where the primary innovation is the METHOD or PROCESS of production rather than the properties of the resulting ingredient. Also includes processes where microbial or mycelial growth is used as a structuring or scaffolding method. Assign Bioprocess design when the primary novelty is HOW the fermentation or production is conducted, even when the output is a specific molecule or ingredient. Does not include variation of cultivation conditions or nutrient regimes where the primary goal is improving ingredient properties, which is Ingredient Optimisation. Does not include improvements to media formulations or testing of novel feedstocks, which is Feedstocks.
- Ingredient optimisation: Inventions for the improvement of individual alternative protein ingredients — including single-cell proteins, mycoprotein, fermentation biomass, and recombinantly expressed proteins, lipids, and other functional components — through any upstream (cultivation conditions, nutrient regimes) or downstream (fractionation, processing, enzymatic treatment) approach where the primary goal is improving ingredient properties, not production yield or efficiency. Covers improvements to functional properties, sensory qualities, nutritional profile, and digestibility. Prefer over End Product Formulation when a single ingredient is the primary object of the invention. Does not cover production methods where the primary novelty is the process itself, which is Bioprocess design.
- End product formulation: Inventions for the design and assembly of finished alternative protein products, focused on product composition and properties. Covers multi-ingredient formulation decisions, fat integration, shelf-life and stability, and whole-product nutritional targets and fortification. Prefer this category over Ingredient Optimisation when a finished formulation is the primary object of the invention. A patent that claims a product COMPOSITION — where texture is one property of the claimed formula rather than the primary process innovation — belongs here, not in Texturization methods.
- Texturization methods: Inventions for process innovations or apparatus aimed at improving the texture of finished alternative protein products, including but not limited to extrusion, electrospinning, 3D printing, and enzymatic processing. Also includes ingredients specifically extracted or processed where the primary stated function is texturization (e.g. a protein extract for coagulation or gelling) — prefer Texturization methods over Ingredient optimisation when texturizing function is the primary purpose of the ingredient. Does not include methods for studying or modifying individual ingredients for non-texture purposes, which is Ingredient Optimisation.
- Food safety & quality: Inventions for improving or ensuring the safety and quality of fermentation-derived alternative protein ingredients and products, including novel preservation methods, antimicrobial treatments, allergen detection or reduction methods, and novel analytical methods or assays for quality or regulatory compliance. Does not include the assessment of process safety, occupational safety, or environmental safety of manufacturing facilities, cleaning protocols, or equipment maintenance.
- Health & nutrition: Inventions specifically designed to improve the nutritional composition, digestibility, or health-relevant properties of fermentation-derived alternative protein ingredients or products — such as novel processes to reduce anti-nutritional factors, increase protein digestibility or bioavailability, enhance micronutrient content, or achieve specific nutritional targets. Does not include general nutritional characterisation or profiling conducted as part of ingredient or product development, which belongs in Ingredient Optimisation or End Product Formulation.
- Other: Catch-all for patents that do not cleanly fit into the specific categories above, including: patents spanning multiple categories without a clear dominant focus; patents describing general fermentation or manufacturing infrastructure not specific to alternative protein production; patents covering packaging, distribution, or storage of alternative protein products; and inventions not covered by the other categories.
