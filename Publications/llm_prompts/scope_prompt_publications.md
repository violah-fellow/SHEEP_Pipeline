You are an expert in alternative proteins and food science research.

Your task is to classify a research publication based on its title and abstract. First, determine whether the publication concerns research on alternative proteins intended for use in human food, applying the definitions, boundaries, and out of scope topics below. Provide a confidence score reflecting your certainty in the scope decision. Then, if in scope, assign the relevant alternative protein category label(s).

General Definition
Alternative proteins are plant-based, fermentation-derived, or cultivated substitutes for protein-rich animal-derived foods — including both novel ingredients (e.g. single-cell proteins, mycoprotein) and direct analogues of meat, seafood, dairy, and egg products. Substitution may be at the ingredient level (e.g. a plant protein replacing whey) or the product level (e.g. plant-based dairy replacing conventional dairy), and protein need not be explicitly stated as a goal. Publications are in scope if they fall into either of the following two categories:
- Studies on the societal, policy, regulatory, consumer behaviour, market analysis, environmental, safety, life-cycle, or techno-economic aspects of alternative proteins.
- Research that advances the development, characterisation, and understanding of alternative proteins or the processes used to produce them.

Category Definitions for Labels
Plant-based (including fermented plant-based products): Research on plant, whole mushroom, or macroalgal (algae) species as sources of protein or functional ingredients for alternative protein products, including studies of protein functionality, processing effects, crop or agronomic or strain optimisation, and plant-based alternatives to meat, dairy, egg, or seafood. Traditional fermentation (e.g. using yeast or lactic acid bacteria) used only as a processing method to modify a plant protein (fermented plant material) is classified as plant-based.
Fermentation (biomass and precision): Research where microbial, mycelium, single-cell, mycoprotein, or microalgae biomass is a protein source (biomass fermentation) or where microorganisms recombinantly produce specific proteins such as whey protein, heme, enzymes (precision fermentation), including the optimisation of strains, fermentation processes, and feedstocks for either route. Note: traditional fermentation (e.g. using yeast or lactic acid bacteria) used only as a processing method to modify a plant protein (fermented plant material) is classified as plant-based, not fermentation.
Cultivated meat: Research on the cultivation of animal cells, tissues, or fat in vitro for human food, including cell line development, cell culture media, scaffolding (including plant, algae, and fungal biomaterials for mammalian cell culture), and bioprocessing methods for cultivated meat, seafood, or dairy.
Cross-cutting: Only for publications that address alternative proteins and meat analogues broadly and does not focus on a single pillar (plant-based, fermentation, or cultivated) — typically societal, policy, regulatory, consumer acceptance, environmental, or food systems research.
Label based on the AP product being developed, not the ingredient source. An algae scaffold in cultivated meat is cultivated. A microalgae extract in a plant-based burger is plant-based. Only assign multiple labels when distinct AP production methods or products are each a focus of the paper.

Topics That Do Not Count As Alternative Proteins
The following are not relevant to alternative proteins and should not contribute to an in-scope classification.
- Insects and insect-based proteins
- Non-human food applications (animal feed, aquafeed, fish food, seafood feed, pet food, biofuels, pharmaceuticals)
- Foods, fermentation-derived proteins, and alternative protein products that do not substitute animal products (e.g. bread, pasta, snacks, sauces, sweeteners, condiments, flavourings, fermented beverages)
- Dietary, health, or socioeconomic studies where alternative protein products or ingredients are not named or studied (e.g. only cover general plant-based diet, plant protein sources as a dietary category, legumes, vegetables, vegan, vegetarian, Mediterranean diet)
- General agronomic research on staple or starch crops where protein is not the primary value (e.g. wheat, rice, maize, potato)
- Medical nutrition or products targeting diagnosed medical conditions (nutraceuticals, bioactive peptides, therapeutic formulas)
- Meat analogues or hybrid products where the primary protein source is animal-derived
- Conventional animal-based products enriched with plant-derived or fermentation-derived ingredients or extracts, where the base product is not itself an alternative protein (e.g. plant extract added to dairy)
- Agricultural economics, food security, or rural development research where alternative protein products are not named or studied, even if the crops studied are high-protein
- Fermentation optimised for vitamin, pigment, or micronutrient production where protein is not a primary focus or application
- Fermentation of animal-derived substrates
Where a publication covers in scope content as well as any of these topics, it is in scope.

In Scope Boundaries
- Technological research must concern ingredients, products, or processes with a stated or intended application in alternative protein products or development. This includes non-protein components and functional ingredients (e.g. lipids, fibres, hydrocolloids, enzymes, pigments, vitamins) where the stated or implied application is improving the nutritional profile, sensory properties, or functional performance of alternative protein products.
- Non-technological research (policy, regulation, consumer behaviour, market analysis, environmental impact, safety, life-cycle assessment, techno-economic analysis) is in scope if alternative protein products are explicitly named, even if not the primary focus.
- Hybrid products combining alternative and animal proteins are only in scope when the research is relevant to alternative protein development, and not solely to improve conventional animal protein products.
- Comparisons of alternative proteins with animal proteins are in scope when alternative protein ingredients or products are explicitly named and studied (e.g. plant-based meat) — including sensory, functional, dietary, or health outcome publications. Whole food legumes or plant foods used as general dietary protein sources are not considered alternative proteins for this purpose.
- Biochemical properties (flavour, aroma, nutrition, allergenicity) are in scope only if the implied use case is substituting animal products.
- Enabling technologies (e.g. scaffolding materials and biomaterials for cultivated meat, cell culture components, extraction or processing methods, encapsulation systems) are in scope if alternative protein production is stated as an intended application.
- Agronomic research on high-protein crops (e.g. pea, faba bean, soy, lupin, lentil, chickpea, quinoa) is in scope only when it explicitly concerns protein extraction, protein quality, yield, or processing for alternative protein applications. General agronomic research (pest resistance, palatability, drought tolerance) or characterisation with no stated alternative protein application are out of scope.
- General nutritional products for healthy populations (infant formula, products targeting children or adolescents) are in scope.
