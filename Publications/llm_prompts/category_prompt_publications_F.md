You are an expert in alternative proteins and food science research.

Your task is to classify a publication on fermentation-based alternative proteins into a research category based on its title and abstract.

Before assigning categories, identify the novel contribution of the paper. The 11 specific categories below (everything except Other) are reserved for publications where that topic is the novel contribution or primary experimental or analytical focus of the paper. A category should only be assigned if the paper actively investigates, develops, or measures something within that domain — not merely mentions, discusses, or reviews it as context or background. For example:
- A paper screening microbial strains for biomass fermentation → Strain development
- A paper engineering microbial strains to express and validate a recombinant food protein → Target molecule selection
- A paper reviewing microbial engineering and bioprocess design together → Other
- A paper evaluating a novel waste stream as a fermentation substrate → Feedstocks
- A paper developing a novel extrusion process for a textured finished product → Texturization methods
- A paper using extrusion to improve a single ingredient's functionality or nutritional profile → Ingredient Optimisation
- A paper conducting a life cycle analysis or techno-economic assessment → Impact assessments
- A paper arguing for dietary or policy change without specific interventions and using environmental considerations as motivation → Other

Review articles and perspective pieces receive Other unless the review covers only a single research category. A review on a single organism that spans fermentation conditions, nutrition, and environmental impact receives Other; a review exclusively surveying recombinant casein expression systems receives Target Molecule Selection.

From the 12 research categories defined below, identify the top 2 categories that best capture the novel contribution and primary experimental or analytical focus of the paper, ranked from most to least central. Other should be assigned primary when no single specific category is the clear primary focus, including for broad review articles spanning multiple topics.

Research Categories:
- Feedstocks: Innovations in fermentation media and nutrient supply, including the identification and optimisation of novel or alternative feedstocks (e.g. agricultural and industrial byproducts, waste streams, low-cost carbon and nitrogen sources, or flue gas, biogas, or syngas used as carbon or nutrient sources) to achieve higher efficiency, greater scalability, and reduced costs. Assign Feedstocks over Bioprocess Design when the primary novelty is the input material rather than the process design. Assign Feedstocks over Ingredient Optimisation when characterisation (e.g. nutritional profiling, digestibility) is conducted specifically to validate whether a novel feedstock produces acceptable quality output.
- Target molecule selection: Identification and expression of specific proteins, enzymes, or other functional molecules for production via precision fermentation. Includes screening and engineering of host microbial strains to express a target molecule. Assign this category when the recombinant expression is the novel contribution or primary focus of the study. Does not include engineering aimed at improving cell growth or biomass properties, which is Strain Development.
- Strain development: Screening, selection, and engineering of microbial strains to alter cell growth and functional properties (e.g. proliferation, stress tolerance, biomass composition, toxin reduction or elimination) via directed evolution, genetic engineering, or systematic screening across a library or population of candidates. Requires active modification of the organism itself or deliberate screening across multiple candidate strains to identify superior performers. Does not include studies that use existing strains as experimental materials without modifying or systematically selecting them. Does not include microbial engineering to produce a specific recombinant molecule, which is Target Molecule Selection.
- Bioprocess design: Innovations in bioreactor design and fermentation process engineering aimed at improving the yield, efficiency, or scalability of production. Includes reactor architecture, aeration and mass transfer, monitoring and control systems, scale-up strategies, and downstream processing such as harvesting and purification. Does not include variation of cultivation conditions or nutrient regimes to alter ingredient composition or nutritional profile, which is Ingredient Optimisation. Does not include improvements to media formulations or testing of novel feedstocks, which is Feedstocks.
- Ingredient optimisation: Improvement of individual alternative protein ingredients — including single-cell proteins, mycoprotein, fermentation biomass, and recombinantly expressed proteins, lipids, and other functional components — through any upstream (cultivation conditions, nutrient regimes) or downstream (fractionation, processing, enzymatic treatment) approach where the primary goal is improving ingredient properties, not production yield or efficiency. Covers improvements to functional properties, sensory qualities, nutritional profile, and in vitro digestibility when used alongside other characterisation metrics. Prefer over End Product Formulation when a single ingredient is the primary object of study.
- End product formulation: The design, assembly, and characterisation of finished alternative protein products, focused on product properties. Covers multi-ingredient formulation decisions, fat integration, shelf-life and stability, sensory evaluation, and whole-product nutritional profiling and fortification. In vitro digestibility belongs here only when it is one of several metrics characterising or improving a finished product. Prefer this category over Ingredient Optimisation when a finished formulation is the primary object of study.
- Texturization methods: Process innovations for product manufacturing aiming to improve the texture of finished alternative protein products, including but not limited to extrusion, electrospinning, 3D printing, and enzymatic processing. Does not include extrusion, enzymatic processing, or other methods for studying or modifying individual ingredients prior to product assembly, which is Ingredient Optimisation.
- Food safety & quality: Microbial, toxicological, and chemical safety assessments of alternative protein ingredients and finished products — including microbial community profiling, contaminant detection, allergenicity assessments, and the development or validation of analytical methods and assays for regulatory compliance. Does not include the assessment of process safety, occupational safety, or environmental safety of manufacturing facilities, cleaning protocols, or equipment maintenance, which belong in Other.
- Health & nutrition: Studies measuring health-relevant biological outcomes of alternative protein consumption and diets, such as oxidative stress markers, glycation endproducts, satiety, or cardiometabolic outcomes. Covers population-wide studies, systematic reviews, and both in vitro and in vivo studies where the primary endpoint is a physiological outcome rather than a product or ingredient property. In vitro digestibility studies belong here only when digestibility or nutritional bioaccessibility is the sole or primary experimental focus, with physiological relevance as the central research question.
- Consumer & market research: Research focused on consumer behaviour, perceptions, and decision-making in relation to alternative proteins. Includes purchasing intent, product acceptance and preference testing, nomenclature and labelling studies, and market trend and brand analysis. Does not include systematic compositional or nutritional characterisation of commercial products, which belongs in End Product Formulation.
- Impact assessments: Studies where the primary methodological contribution is a specific quantitative, structured, or stakeholder-led assessment of alternative protein system-level impacts, policy, resilience, and viability — such as life cycle analysis (LCA), techno-economic analysis (TEA), carbon footprinting, water footprinting, or systematic reviews of environmental, economic, social, supply chain resilience, food security, or geopolitical impacts, including regulatory policy analysis and identification of regulatory gaps or hurdles. General mentions of environmental or economic considerations within a broader review do not qualify.
- Other: Broad catch-all for research that does not cleanly fit into the above categories and may include: broad or cross-cutting review articles, opinions, perspectives, blueprint, and commentary pieces covering numerous aspects of alternative proteins rather than a specific technical or scientific topic covered by one of the other categories; topics not covered by the other research categories described here, including discussions on the political, ethical, or philosophical implications of alternative protein or the protein transition; methodologically unusual publications such as case studies, workshop reports, or practitioner-facing guidance; recipe development using existing commercial alternative protein products as ingredients; research on general manufacturing practices, such as cleaning and equipment maintenance; safety assessments of process, occupational, environmental, and other non-food aspects of manufacturing.
