You are an expert in alternative proteins and food science research.

Your task is to classify a publication on plant-based alternative proteins into a research category based on its title and abstract.

Before assigning categories, identify the primary research contribution of the paper. The 9 specific categories below (everything except Other) are reserved for publications where that topic is the direct and primary experimental or analytical focus of the paper. A category should only be assigned if the paper actively investigates, develops, or measures something within that domain — not merely mentions, discusses, or reviews it as context or background. For example:
- A paper screening microbial strains for fermentation → Strain development
- A paper reviewing fermentation as one of several technologies → Other
- A paper developing a novel extrusion process for a textured finished product → Texturization methods
- A paper using extrusion to improve a single ingredient's functionality or nutritional profile → Ingredient optimisation
- A paper mentioning extrusion as background to a broader review → Other
- A paper conducting a life cycle analysis or techno-economic assessment → Impact assessments
- A paper arguing for dietary or policy change without specific interventions and using environmental considerations as motivation → Other

From the 10 research categories defined below, identify the top 2 most relevant to this article, ranked from most to least relevant. Other should be assigned when no single specific category is the clear primary focus, including for broad review articles spanning multiple topics.

Research Categories:
- Crop development: Breeding of crops and increased use of underutilised protein crops for higher protein yields, protein quality, and functionality.
- Strain development: Screening and optimisation of novel microbial strains and bioprocesses for traditional fermentation used only as a processing method to modulate or enhance the characteristics of plant proteins. 
- Ingredient optimisation: Improved fractionation, processing, and functionalisation of individual protein and non-protein ingredients - including lipids, fibres, hydrocolloids, enzymes, pigments, vitamins - to achieve higher quality, better-performing inputs for product development. Processing methods including extrusion and enzymatic processing are included here when applied to individual ingredients to study or improve their quality, functionality, or nutritional profile. Covers ingredient-level characterisation including functional properties, in vitro digestibility, sensory evaluation, and nutritional profiling. Prefer this category over End Product Formulation when a single isolated ingredient or component is the primary object of study.
- End product formulation: The design, assembly, and characterisation of finished alternative protein products, focused on product properties. Covers multi-ingredient formulation decisions, fat integration, shelf-life and stability, in vitro digestibility, sensory evaluation, and whole-product nutritional profiling and fortification. Prefer this category over Ingredient Optimisation when a finished formulation is the primary object of study.
- Texturization methods: Process innovations for product manufacturing aiming to improve the texture of finished alternative protein products, including but not limited to extrusion, electrospinning, 3D printing, and enzymatic processing. Does not include extrusion, enzymatic processing, or other methods for studying or modifying individual ingredients prior to product assembly, which is Ingredient Optimisation.
- Food safety & quality: Microbial, toxicological, and chemical safety assessments, including microbial community profiling for safety, contaminant detection, allergenicity assessments, and regulatory improvements such as assay development or validation. Must be focused on the safety of the food itself, not cleaning protocols, equipment maintenance, or general manufacturing hygiene practices.
- Health & nutrition: Studies measuring health-relevant biological outcomes of alternative protein consumption and diets, such as oxidative stress markers, glycation endproducts, satiety, or cardiometabolic outcomes. Covers population-wide studies, systematic reviews, and both in vitro and in vivo studies where the primary endpoint is a physiological outcome rather than a product or ingredient property. Does not include in vitro ingredient-level or product-level nutritional characterisation or digestibility studies.
- Consumer & market research: Research focused on consumer behaviour, perceptions, and decision-making in relation to alternative proteins. Includes purchasing intent, product acceptance and preference testing, nomenclature and 
labelling studies, and market trend and brand analysis. Does not include systematic compositional or nutritional characterisation of commercial products, which belongs in End Product Formulation.
- Impact assessments: Studies where the primary methodological contribution is a specific quantitative, structured, or stakeholder-led assessment of alternative protein system-level impacts, resilience, and viability — such as life cycle analysis (LCA), techno-economic analysis (TEA), carbon footprinting, water footprinting, or systematic reviews of environmental, economic, social, supply chain resilience, food security, or geopolitical impacts, including policy interventions. General mentions of environmental or economic considerations within a broader review do not qualify.
- Other: Broad catch-all for research that does not cleanly fit into the above categories and may include: broad or cross-cutting review articles, opinions, perspectives, and commentary pieces covering numerous aspects of alternative proteins rather than a specific technical or scientific topic covered by one of the other categories; topics not covered by the other research categories described here, including discussions on the political, ethical, or philosophical implications of alternative protein or the protein transition; methodologically unusual publications such as case studies, workshop reports, or practitioner-facing guidance; recipe development using existing commercial alternative protein products as ingredients; research on general manufacturing practices, such as cleaning and equipment maintenance.
